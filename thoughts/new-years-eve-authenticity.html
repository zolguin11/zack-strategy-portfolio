Longer thought

New Year's Eve and the search for something real.

When the official party starts to feel fake, people do not stop wanting ritual. They start looking for a weirder one that belongs to them.

Culture, ritual, and unauthorized meaning

I keep thinking about New Year's Eve as a strategy problem, which is admittedly a strange sentence to write. But it is one of the clearest examples of something I keep noticing: people do not reject meaning. They reject meaning that has been emptied out, overproduced, and sold back to them with a logo on top.

The obvious version is Times Square. It is supposed to be the center of the ritual: the countdown, the ball drop, the crowd, the kiss, the country watching together. But the more I look at it, the less it feels like a party and the more it feels like a simulation of one. A public square has been converted into a broadcast asset. Every angle has a sponsor. Every tradition has a camera position. Even the spontaneity has the feeling of something that has already been cleared by legal.

And maybe that is why it feels so thin. Not because people are incapable of celebration now. Not because everyone forgot how to be together. I think that diagnosis is too easy, and probably too flattering to the past. The better explanation is that people can tell when a ritual is no longer for them.

The fake party and the real one

What I love about the Los Angeles Smoking Deaths billboard countdown is that it is almost comically not designed to be a New Year's Eve destination. It is a public-health counter. It is earnest, grim, a little awkward, and weirdly human. Then, once a year, people gather around it to watch the number reset.

That is the kind of cultural glitch I am interested in. It was not focus-grouped into existence. Nobody sat in a room and said, “How do we activate young urban consumers around mortality awareness at midnight?” It happened because people saw something strange, felt the possibility of a ritual inside it, and collectively decided to show up.

The meaning is layered in a way that no planned brand activation could survive. It is funny. It is bleak. It is sincere. It is a crowd cheering for a counter that is also, literally, counting human loss. Then the number turns over, and everyone gets to feel the absurdity together. That combination of irony and sincerity feels very now. Not empty irony. Protective irony. The kind people use when they still want to feel something, but do not fully trust the world not to monetize their feelings.

Authenticity is not a visual style

Brands talk about authenticity constantly, but often as if it is an aesthetic. Make it raw. Make it handheld. Use a less polished font. Put a little dust on the edges. But authenticity is not a texture. It is a relationship between intent and participation.

Times Square has the visual ingredients of celebration, but the intent feels captured. The billboard party has none of the official ingredients, but the participation feels real. That matters. People are increasingly skilled at detecting the difference between a space that welcomes them and a space that extracts from them.

This is also why roadtrip stops make sense to me: Salvation Mountain, Carhenge, Prada Marfa, and all the other strange places that make a trip feel specific instead of merely efficient. They are rarely the most polished thing you see. That is part of the point. They give the journey a texture that the interstate and the algorithm would never choose for you.

Culture leaks through the optimized world

The internet trained us to find and amplify weirdness. For a while, that felt freeing. Then the digital spaces where that weirdness lived started to feel more like shopping malls: optimized, ad-loaded, flattened, and built around turning attention into someone else's asset.

So now the pendulum is moving again. People are looking for physical proof that culture is still theirs. Not necessarily in grand, organized, institutionally approved ways. More like: meet me at the strange billboard, the desert art project, the cars half-buried in a Nebraska field, the luxury-store mirage in West Texas, the place that became meaningful because somebody cared enough to make it real and strange.

I find that hopeful. It means the instinct is still alive. We still want shared rituals. We still want to look side to side and feel that other people are in on the same strange little moment. We still know, somewhere internally, when something real is happening.

The strategy lesson

For me, the lesson is not “brands should make weirder stunts.” That would miss the point and probably ruin the thing it is trying to copy. The lesson is that people are starving for spaces where they are not merely the audience, the data source, or the monetizable crowd. They want to be participants in meaning.

Good strategy should respect that. It should know when to design and when to leave room. It should know when a brand can host culture and when it should simply not interfere. It should understand that the most valuable thing about a gathering might be the value no single stakeholder gets to capture.

Empty traditions do not mean people are empty. They mean the tradition stopped carrying the feeling it was supposed to carry. And when that happens, people will crawl under the fence of the optimized world and find another place to stand.

That is what makes the weird New Year's Eve party beautiful to me. Not the billboard itself, exactly. The authorship. The reminder that culture is not only something delivered to us. It is something we keep making, often clumsily, often ironically, often on a dirty sidewalk, but still together.
